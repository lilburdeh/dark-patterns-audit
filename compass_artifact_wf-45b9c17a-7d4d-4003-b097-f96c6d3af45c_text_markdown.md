# Gray et al.'s dark patterns ontology is the right foundation, but not sufficient alone

**Gray et al. (2024) is the best single framework for BIT's compliance-checking application — but a hybrid approach would be materially stronger.** The ontology's unique three-level hierarchy (5 high-level strategies, 25 meso-level patterns, 35 low-level patterns) was explicitly built by harmonizing 10 academic and regulatory taxonomies, making it the only framework that bridges detection-ready UI-level patterns with policy-targetable strategies. For a regulatory compliance tool, this bridging function is decisive. However, the ontology has notable gaps in operationalization and empirical validation that Mathur et al. (2019) and emerging LLM-based detection tools address better. The optimal architecture for BIT is Gray et al.'s ontology as the master classification schema, supplemented by Mathur et al.'s operationalized definitions for text-based detection and the EDPB/CMA taxonomies for jurisdiction-specific regulatory reporting.

## How Gray et al. (2024) was built and what it contains

The ontology — formally titled "An Ontology of Dark Patterns Knowledge" and published at CHI '24 by Colin Gray, Nataliia Bielova, Cristiana Santos, and Thomas Mildner — emerged from a qualitative content analysis of **262 dark pattern descriptions** drawn from five academic sources (Brignull, Gray et al. 2018, Mathur et al. 2019, Luguri & Strahilevitz 2021, Bösch et al. 2016) and five regulatory sources (EDPB, EU Commission, OECD, UK CMA, FTC). The researchers aggregated all patterns verbatim, traced citation provenance, clustered similar patterns, and introduced a novel "meso" level to bridge abstract strategies and concrete UI elements.

The resulting ontology contains **65 distinct pattern types** organized into three tiers. The five high-level strategies — **Sneaking, Obstruction, Interface Interference, Forced Action, and Social Engineering** — describe broad manipulation approaches targetable by policy. Twenty-five meso-level patterns (such as Roach Motel, Bad Defaults, Scarcity Claims, Shaming) describe specific mechanisms of influence. Thirty-five low-level patterns (such as Countdown Timers, Confirmshaming, Disguised Ads, Privacy Zuckering) describe concrete UI implementations amenable to automated detection. Each level uses a standardized definition syntax that specifies the UI element altered, the incorrect user expectation created, and the undesired effect produced.

The ontology's key structural innovation is that **low-level patterns map upward through meso to high-level patterns**, allowing a detection tool to flag a specific UI element (e.g., a countdown timer) and automatically connect it to the relevant regulatory concept (social engineering via urgency). This is precisely what a compliance-checking tool needs. However, a significant limitation is that **many meso-level patterns lack defined low-level instances** — Nagging, Trick Questions, Choice Overload, Bad Defaults, Hidden Information, Feedforward Ambiguity, Forced Continuity, Forced Registration, and Personalization all exist at the meso level without concrete low-level detection targets. The authors acknowledge this requires future empirical work to fill.

Validation relies on **face validity through community member-checking** with a Slack community of 100+ transdisciplinary researchers. No formal inter-rater reliability study has been conducted, which the authors explicitly flag as future work. Since publication, the ontology has been widely adopted: Ahuja et al. (DIS '25) mapped all 59 meso- and low-level patterns to DSA autonomy violation types; Kocyigit et al. (EuroUSEC 2024) extended it with measurable HCI features for cookie consent; and Lin et al. (2026) reference it for LLM-based detection. No published critiques or refutations have appeared.

The table below compares all major academic dark patterns taxonomies against BIT's four priorities:

**Mathur et al. (2019) is Gray et al.'s strongest competitor for detection applications.** Their taxonomy of 15 types across 7 categories was derived directly from an automated crawl of 53,000 product pages across 11,000 shopping websites — making it the most empirically grounded framework in the field. Their open dataset of 1,818 labelled dark pattern instances is the foundation used by nearly every subsequent detection tool. BERT/RoBERTa classifiers trained on this dataset achieve **97.5% accuracy** on text-based pattern detection. Brignull himself uses the Mathur taxonomy for expert witness work. The limitation is domain scope: it covers e-commerce text patterns but misses visual manipulation, privacy dark patterns, social media engagement patterns, and many mobile-specific techniques.

The **DPAF (Dark Pattern Analysis Framework)** from a December 2024 arXiv paper identifies 68 unique types — slightly more than Gray et al.'s 65 — validated through industry surveys. Critically, it found that eight existing detection tools could only identify **31 of 68 types (45.5% coverage)**, exposing significant gaps in current operationalization regardless of which taxonomy is used. This suggests the comprehensiveness advantage of any single framework is partly theoretical until detection capabilities catch up.

**Luguri & Strahilevitz (2021)** offers something no other framework provides: experimental evidence of effectiveness. Their controlled experiments showed dark patterns increased unwanted acceptance rates from **11.3% (control) to 25.4% (mild) to 37.2% (aggressive)**. Their mild-versus-aggressive severity classification is directly relevant to regulatory enforcement, which must assess harm levels, but it requires subjective contextual judgment that resists automation.

## Regulatory frameworks and how Gray et al. maps to them

Gray et al.'s ontology was explicitly constructed to harmonize regulatory and academic terminology — a design choice that gives it a structural advantage over every competing framework for compliance applications. The six major regulatory frameworks each take different approaches:

The **OECD (2022)** is the most naturally aligned, using the same five high-level category names from Gray et al. (2018) — Nagging, Obstruction, Sneaking, Interface Interference, Forced Action — plus Social Proof and Urgency from Mathur et al. The OECD taxonomy was used as the classification framework for the **2024 GPEN/ICPEN coordinated sweep** of 1,010+ websites across 26 privacy and 27 consumer protection authorities, finding 97% had at least one deceptive design pattern.

The **EDPB Guidelines 03/2022** created a novel six-category taxonomy (Overloading, Skipping, Stirring, Obstructing, Fickle, Left in the Dark) with approximately 15 specific types, focused entirely on data protection in social media. This was one of Gray et al.'s 10 source taxonomies and maps imperfectly: EDPB's "Overloading" spans Gray et al.'s Obstruction and Interface Interference; "Stirring" maps to Social Engineering and Interface Interference. The EDPB's taxonomy is **the most operationally detailed** regulatory framework, with extensive examples, use-case walkthroughs, and a compliance checklist annex.

The **EU DSA Article 25** prohibits practices that "deceive or manipulate" or "materially distort or impair" autonomous decision-making, but provides only a handful of specific examples in Recital 67 rather than a comprehensive typology. Santos, Bielova, and Ahuja mapped all 59 meso- and low-level Gray et al. patterns to three DSA autonomy violation types (deception, manipulation, distortion/impairment), demonstrating that the ontology can serve as the interpretive bridge the DSA itself lacks. The forthcoming **EU Digital Fairness Act** (expected Q4 2026) may be the first binding legislation to formally incorporate a multi-level classification structure.

The **UK CMA's Online Choice Architecture** framework is the most analytically rigorous regulatory taxonomy, with 21 practices across three categories (Choice Structure, Choice Information, Choice Pressure) backed by a detailed evidence review. The CMA's broader "choice architecture" framing captures concepts like anchoring, decoys, and sunk costs that are less explicit in Gray et al.'s design-focused ontology. The **Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024** now gives the CMA direct enforcement powers with fines up to 10% of global turnover, making CMA alignment particularly important for UK-focused work.

The **FTC's 2022 report** is the most academically grounded regulatory document, explicitly citing Gray et al. (2018), Mathur et al. (2019), and Luguri & Strahilevitz (2021). India's CCPA (2023) provides the most specific enumerated list with 13 named dark patterns. California's CPRA takes a principles-based approach focused on autonomy subversion rather than cataloguing types.

For BIT's compliance tool, the critical insight is that **no regulatory framework has adopted Gray et al.'s three-level hierarchy**, but Gray et al.'s ontology is the only framework that was built to map across all of them. A compliance tool using Gray et al. can flag a low-level pattern and generate regulatory-specific reporting by mapping upward through the hierarchy to the relevant CMA, EDPB, DSA, or FTC category.

## What BIT already uses and the practitioner landscape

**BIT does not have a published standalone dark patterns taxonomy.** BIT's approach draws from CMA, Brignull, and Mathur taxonomies, adapted for specific regulatory auditing contexts. BIT's most notable methodological contribution is the **Behavioural Risk Audit** — a checklist-led structured observation framework tested on 10 UK gambling platforms in 2022 — which has been cited by Ofcom and academic researchers as a model for regulatory auditing. BIT has also proposed a simplified consumer-facing framework of "Detours, Roundabouts, and Shortcuts" that describes dark patterns by their psychological process rather than UI manifestation.

BIT's existing work is primarily methodological (how to audit) rather than taxonomic (what to classify). This means adopting Gray et al.'s ontology would complement rather than conflict with BIT's established approach — the ontology provides the classification schema, while BIT's audit methodology provides the process for applying it.

In the broader practitioner landscape, **Deceptive.design** (Brignull's site) currently lists 16 types but its founder endorses Mathur et al.'s taxonomy for professional work. **Fair Patterns** (fairpatterns.com) offers the only commercial AI-powered compliance platform, running 70+ checks per screen with a paired dark-pattern/fair-pattern framework explicitly aligned with GDPR, DSA, and the AI Act. **WCAG 3.0** is developing an "Avoid Deception" guideline that would be the first international web standard addressing dark patterns, though finalization is not expected before 2028. Google published a framework in September 2025 proposing a two-dimensional assessment (mechanism deceptiveness × impact severity), though its industry provenance warrants caution.

## Detection tools increasingly validate a hybrid approach

The most successful automated detection tools demonstrate that **no single taxonomy dominates operationalization** — the best tools draw from multiple frameworks:

**AutoBot** (Nayak et al., 2025, ACM CCS Distinguished Paper Award) achieves the highest reported performance at **F1 = 0.93** using a two-stage pipeline: YOLOv10 vision ensemble for UI element detection followed by Gemini LLM classification with knowledge distillation to smaller models. It uses Brignull/Mathur categories and integrates into Google Lighthouse as a custom audit — directly usable for compliance scoring. **DPDGPT** (Lin et al., 2025) achieves F1 = 0.88 using a multimodal LLM pipeline explicitly built on a unified taxonomy from Gray et al., Brignull, and Mathur et al., covering **19 dark pattern types** — the widest coverage of any detection tool. The authors designed it specifically for "regulators and consumer protection agencies."

**DPGuard** (2025) takes the most comprehensive approach with 50+ pattern types and a dataset of 6,725 images with 10,421 dark pattern instances. **AppRay** (2024) is the first fully automated end-to-end system handling both app exploration and detection, uniquely capable of identifying **dynamic patterns** (Roach Motel, Bait-and-Switch) that require multi-screen interaction flows.

A critical finding from the DPAF analysis is that existing detection tools collectively cover only **45.5% of known dark pattern types**. This means the detection gap is substantial regardless of which framework is chosen. The practical implication for BIT is that the compliance tool should be designed as a **screening/flagging system that surfaces high-confidence detections for expert review**, not as a definitive classifier. LLM-based approaches are converging as the clear future direction because they can reason across visual and textual features while producing human-readable explanations suitable for compliance documentation.

For text-based detection specifically, the Mathur et al. (2019) dataset remains foundational — fine-tuned RoBERTa achieves **97.5% accuracy** on it — but is limited to e-commerce text patterns. For visual/structural detection, tools built on Gray et al.'s low-level pattern definitions (which specify the UI element altered) are better suited.

## Recommendation: Gray et al. as master schema with tactical supplements

For BIT's policy/regulatory compliance-checking application, the optimal architecture is a **layered hybrid approach** with Gray et al. (2024) as the master classification schema:

- **Classification layer**: Gray et al.'s (2024) three-level ontology as the canonical taxonomy. Its 65 types provide the most comprehensive coverage (priority 1) and its explicit incorporation of regulatory sources provides the strongest regulatory alignment (priority 4). The three-level hierarchy enables the tool to report at the appropriate abstraction level for each regulatory audience — low-level for technical auditors, meso-level for enforcement officers, high-level for policy summaries.

- **Detection layer**: For text-based pattern detection, use Mathur et al.'s (2019) operationalized definitions and dataset as the training foundation. For visual/screenshot-based detection, adopt an AutoBot- or DPDGPT-style multimodal LLM pipeline. These tools achieve the highest empirical performance (priority 2) and practical detection rates (priority 3). Map all detections back to Gray et al.'s ontology categories.

- **Regulatory reporting layer**: Maintain explicit mapping tables from Gray et al.'s ontology to jurisdiction-specific frameworks — EDPB's 6 categories for GDPR contexts, CMA's 21 OCA practices for UK enforcement, DSA Article 25's three autonomy violation types for EU platform regulation, FTC's four categories for US contexts. Ahuja et al. (DIS '25) have already demonstrated this mapping for the DSA; similar exercises are needed for CMA and FTC categories.

- **Gap-filling**: Address Gray et al.'s acknowledged gaps — particularly the nine meso-level patterns without defined low-level instances — by incorporating CMA-specific concepts (anchoring, decoys, sunk costs) and the EDPB's detailed cookie consent guidance. The Potel-Saville "Fair Patterns" approach of pairing each dark pattern with a remediation recommendation adds significant value for a compliance tool that needs to advise on fixes, not just flag problems.

The principal risk of relying solely on Gray et al. is the **validation gap**: the ontology has face validity but no formal inter-rater reliability study, and many meso-level patterns lack concrete detection criteria. Supplementing with Mathur et al.'s empirically grounded definitions and emerging LLM-based detection tools mitigates this. The principal risk of not using Gray et al. is **regulatory fragmentation**: no other framework was built to bridge across the EDPB, CMA, FTC, OECD, and DSA simultaneously.

## Conclusion

Gray et al. (2024) is the correct strategic choice for BIT's application — not because it is perfect, but because it is the only framework architecturally designed to serve the specific function BIT needs: connecting automated detection outputs to multi-jurisdictional regulatory reporting. Its three-level hierarchy is a genuine structural innovation that no competing framework replicates. The ontology's comprehensiveness (65 types synthesized from 262 source patterns across 10 taxonomies) exceeds all alternatives except the December 2024 DPAF (68 types), which lacks Gray et al.'s regulatory provenance mapping and hierarchical structure.

The key actions for BIT are: first, fill the ontology's operationalization gaps by developing detection-ready definitions for the nine undefined meso-level patterns, drawing on CMA and EDPB guidance; second, build jurisdiction-specific mapping tables that translate ontology detections into regulatory language; and third, adopt multimodal LLM-based detection (following AutoBot/DPDGPT architectures) that can leverage Gray et al.'s structured definitions as classification prompts. This hybrid approach — Gray et al.'s ontology as the taxonomic backbone, Mathur et al.'s datasets for training, and LLM pipelines for detection — represents the most defensible, comprehensive, and practically effective architecture available for dark patterns compliance checking in 2026.
